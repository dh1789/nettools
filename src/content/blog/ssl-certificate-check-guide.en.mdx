## Certificate Expiry Sneaks Up on You

One of the most deflating outages is an **expired SSL certificate**. The code and the server are fine, yet one day the browser throws a "Your connection is not private" warning and users leave. Even with auto-renewal, a silent renewal failure leads to the same incident.

That's why certificates need **regular, hands-on checks**. This guide covers the key fields of an SSL certificate and how to inspect them.

## Key Fields to Check in an SSL Certificate

A certificate has many fields, but from an operations standpoint these are the must-checks:

- **Validity (Not Before / Not After)**: the expiry date is what matters. Teams typically alert from 30 days out.
- **Issuer**: which CA issued it — Let's Encrypt, DigiCert, and so on.
- **Subject / SAN**: the domains the certificate protects, including `www` and wildcards (`*.example.com`).
- **Certificate chain**: whether the trust chain to the root CA is intact. A missing intermediate is a common mistake.
- **Signature algorithm**: SHA-256 or stronger (SHA-1 is deprecated).

## Checking a Certificate Step by Step

Enter a domain into the [SSL Checker](/tools/net/ssl-checker/) and you can see all of the above at a glance.

1. Enter the domain to inspect (e.g. `example.com`).
2. Check the expiry date and **days remaining**. Under 30 days? Review your renewal schedule.
3. Confirm the issuer and SAN list include all the domains you intend.
4. Verify the certificate chain is complete. Some clients (especially mobile and certain bots) fail to connect if an intermediate certificate is missing.

If you prefer the command line, OpenSSL is handy:

```bash
echo | openssl s_client -servername example.com -connect example.com:443 2>/dev/null \
  | openssl x509 -noout -dates -issuer -subject
```

## A Routine to Prevent Expiry Incidents

- **Monitoring**: alert N days before expiry (e.g. 30/14/7 days). Certificate expiry is about the only outage you can fully predict in advance.
- **Verify auto-renewal**: if you use Let's Encrypt auto-renewal, monitor whether the renewal **succeeded**, not just that it ran.
- **Chain checks**: when replacing a certificate, confirm the intermediate certificates were deployed too.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

### Q. If the browser padlock is closed, is it safe?

The padlock only means "the connection is encrypted." A certificate that's about to expire or has an incomplete chain may still pass in your current browser. That's why you check expiry and chain separately.

### Q. Does a wildcard certificate protect all subdomains?

`*.example.com` covers only one level of subdomain. Multi-level names like `a.b.example.com` need a separate certificate or SAN. Use the [SSL Checker](/tools/net/ssl-checker/) to see the actual coverage in the SAN list.

### Q. Can't I just temporarily ignore an expired certificate?

Showing users a security warning directly damages trust. Renewing is faster and safer than any workaround.
