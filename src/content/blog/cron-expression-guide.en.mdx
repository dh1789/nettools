## What is a Cron Expression?

Cron is a scheduler in Unix/Linux systems that runs tasks at specified times. A cron expression defines **"when to execute"** using 5 fields.

```
┌───────── Minute (0-59)
│ ┌─────── Hour (0-23)
│ │ ┌───── Day of Month (1-31)
│ │ │ ┌─── Month (1-12)
│ │ │ │ ┌─ Day of Week (0-7, both 0 and 7 = Sunday)
│ │ │ │ │
* * * * *
```

## Special Characters

| Char | Meaning | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| `*`  | Any value | `* * * * *` = every minute |
| `,`  | List | `1,15 * * * *` = at minute 1 and 15 |
| `-`  | Range | `1-5 * * * *` = minutes 1 through 5 |
| `/`  | Step | `*/10 * * * *` = every 10 minutes |

## Common Patterns in Practice

### Run at Midnight Daily

```
0 0 * * *
```

The most popular pattern, used for database backups and log cleanup.

### Weekdays at 9 AM

```
0 9 * * 1-5
```

Great for generating reports or sending notifications at the start of business hours.

### Every 5 Minutes

```
*/5 * * * *
```

Ideal for health checks and monitoring data collection.

### First Day of Every Month at 3 AM

```
0 3 1 * *
```

Used for monthly billing reconciliation and report generation.

### Every Sunday at 2 AM

```
0 2 * * 0
```

Perfect for weekly backups or system maintenance tasks.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Timezone confusion** — Always check your server's timezone (UTC vs local)
2. **Setting both day-of-month and day-of-week** — When both are specified, they work as an OR condition, running more often than expected
3. **Misunderstanding `/` start values** — `5/15` means "every 15 minutes starting at minute 5," not "at minutes 5 and 15"

## Validate Your Cron Expressions

Always verify your cron expressions before deploying. Enter your expression in the [Cron Parser](/tools/net/cron-parser/) to see the next execution times and a human-readable description.

If you need timestamp conversion for your scheduling work, the [Unix Timestamp Converter](/tools/net/unix-timestamp/) is a handy companion tool.

## Summary

Cron expressions only require remembering 5 fields: minute, hour, day, month, and weekday. While they may seem complex at first, learning a few common patterns will make you comfortable in no time.
